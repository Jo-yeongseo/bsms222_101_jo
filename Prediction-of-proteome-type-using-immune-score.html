Proteomic Subtypes Predicted through DEPs Calculated by The Stromal Scores and The Immune Scores

2019250101 Jo yeongseo

2021 12 11

1. Introduction

Our research paper (Chen et al, 2020) studied unique drivers, biomarkers, and mutagenesis through multiomics data of non-smokers lung cancer patients in Taiwan. In addition, through proteome-based classification, they found that some patients in IA and IB stages were divided into ‘late-like’ stages and had clinical implications.

Meanwhile, tumor staging, which is currently being used, is limited in providing cancer prognosis information and cannot predict responses to treatment, so attempts are being made to predict prognosis using other indicators. In some papers, they analyzed stromal scores, immune scores, and life information, and studied how the life can be predicted with the corresponding scores. The stromal and immune scores of tumor and normal were divided into high and low groups, respectively, and DEGs with significant differences between the two groups were selected, and life expectancy was predicted using them.

There is no lifetime information in our dataset, so I was wondering what other information could be grasped through the stromal scores and the immune scores. Now, let’s divide the stromal and immune scores of tumor and normal into high and low groups, respectively, and select DEPs with significant differences between the two groups, and find out information that can be predicted by using them.

  • DEGs: Differentially Expressed Genes.
  • DEPs: Differentially Expressed Proteins.
  • stromal score: quantifying the density and location of stromal cells.
  • immune score: quantifying the density and location of immune cells.

2.2. Find out differentially expressed proteins (DEPs)

There are four groups of the scores - stromal score of normal, immune score of normal, stromal score of tumor, and immune score of tumor. Based on the median of each group, it was divided into high subgroup and low subgroup. In order to find DEPs with significant p-values (p < 0.05) between subgroups in each group, for-loop was created to proceed with the Wilcox test and filter with p < 0.05. 1200 DEPs were found in Stromal Normal, 836 in Immune Normal, 906 in Stromal Tumor, and 412 in Immune Tumor.

The merge function was used to find the DEPs that the four groups have in common, and as a result, three DEPs were found - APH1B, DOCK11, and NENF. In fact, these are the name of the genes, but I will use these like the name of the proteins.

Let’s find out their roles through ‘www.uniprot.org’.

  • APH1B: Probable subunit of the gamma-secretase complex, an endoprotease complex that catalyzes the intramembrane cleavage of integral proteins such as Notch receptors and APP (amyloid-beta precursor protein).
  • DOCK11: Activates CDC42 by exchanging bound GDP for free GTP. Required for marginal zone (MZ) B-cell development, is associated with early bone marrow B-cell development, MZ B-cell formation, MZ B-cell number and marginal metallophilic macrophages morphology. Facilitates filopodia formation through the activation of CDC42.
  • NENF: Promotes cell proliferation and neurogenesis in undifferentiated neural progenitor cells at the embryonic stage and inhibits differentiation of astrocytes.

Through geom_dotplot, the original proteomic type of each patient was represented, and the tertile value of FGD3 log2T/N was calculated to show the red line (33.3% and 66.6%). 3. Conclusion

In our paper, several clinical implications were identified by dividing the proteomic type into three according to the protein pattern, and dividing IA and IB patients into late-like stages. As a result of predicting the proteomic type through DOCK11, a DEP that was identified through the stromal scores and the immune scores, I could predict proteomic types with about 64.62% accuracy. This was the result of using values of only one protein and simply dividing the group by tertile.

DOCK11 used for prediction facilitates filopodia formation through the activation of CDC42. And filopodia stimulate cell migration in many cell types and increased filopodia density has been described in cancer. Several proteins implicated in filopodia formation are relevant for cancer progression, but I suggests that DOCK11 is not only likely to be associated with cancer progression, but also possible to distinguish proteomic types.

Through a follow-up study of finding a larger number of significant proteins and dividing the DOCK11 value more precisely than simply calculated tertile values, a few DEPs may be combined to determine the proteomic type and perform prognostic diagnosis, instead of measuring the expression amount of the entire protein and analyzing the expression pattern to determine the patient’s proteomic type and use it as prognostic factor.
